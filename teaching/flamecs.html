What CS Actually Involves Here

Programming is just the tool, not the discipline. Our CS curriculum goes deep into theory, mathematics, systems, and data science.

Theory

Automata, computability, algorithm analysis, complexity classes

Forms the intellectual core of CS; lets you reason about what can be computed and how efficiently.

Mathematics & Statistics

Discrete math, proofs, linear algebra, probability, statistical inference

Underpins algorithms, machine learning, graphics, cryptography, and more.

Systems

Computer architecture, operating systems, networking, databases

Teaches you how hardware and software interact to create reliable, scalable platforms.

Programming Paradigms

Procedural, object-oriented, functional, concurrent

Gives you versatility to pick the right tool and style for each problem.

Major vs. Minor: The Relationship

Think of the minor as a well-chosen subset of the major, not a "watered-down" version.

Attribute CS Major CS Minor
Credits ~45–50 ~20–24
Depth of Theory Full sequence (discrete math → algorithms → advanced electives) Core theory only (discrete math + algorithms)
Systems Coverage Architecture, OS, networks, databases, distributed systems A subset of that.
Electives 4–6 advanced topics of your choice 1–2 applied electives
Capstone / Research Required Required
Career Trajectory Software engineer, research, grad school, startup founder, Higher studies in CS Technical roles in other disciplines, data-savvy analyst, product manager

Prerequisite Alert: Mathematics

No Math Background = No CS Major or Minor

This is not a scare tactic—it is a realistic warning to prevent frustration and delayed graduation.

Required: Grade 12 Mathematics

Topics should include calculus, algebra/trigonometry, and introductory probability. Without this foundation you will struggle in first-year discrete math and calculus. Moreover, second year courses will also need such mathematical foundations for clear and corrrect understanding.

Why Math Matters in CS

Nearly every CS course builds on mathematical concepts. For example, Algorithm analysis requires calculus and discrete math. Machine learning needs statistics and linear algebra. Graphics requires trigonometry and geometry.

First-Year Course Pathways

First-year structure: You can take 4 courses total - 2 from Physical and Natural Sciences (PNS) and 2 major requirements. CS courses are offered through PNS.

Before selecting any other courses, understand this critical requirement:

MANDATORY CORE REQUIREMENT

CSIT104: Introduction to Computer Science

WITHOUT THIS COURSE, YOU CANNOT TAKE ANY SECOND-YEAR CS COURSES

Available in Term 2 and Term 4 - Choose one offering

Missing CSIT104 = No progression to CS Major/Minor

Strategic Course Selection for CS Success

  • Prioritize CS Courses: Take CSIT104 (mandatory) plus as many other CS courses as possible within your PNS allocation
  • Essential Math Foundation: MATH151 (Introductory Calculus) and MATH155 (Logic & Proofs) are crucial for CS theory
  • Programming Progression: CSIT101 → CSIT111 gives you a strong programming foundation. Have Python experience? Jump to CSIT111
  • Applied Experience: CSIT121 (Computational Modeling) and CSIT122 (IoT) show real-world CS applications
  • Recommendation: If serious about CS, aim for 2-3 CS courses + 1-2 essential math courses in your first year

Remember: Maximize Your CS Exposure

Since CS courses are available through PNS, students committed to CS should take as many CS offerings as possible rather than spreading across other PNS options. This strategic selection gives you the strongest foundation for advanced CS coursework.

Self-Assessment Checklist

Answer yes to every question before declaring CS MAJOR and MINOR

  • Did I enjoy and succeed in Grade 12 math?
  • Am I comfortable writing logical proofs or willing to learn quickly?
  • Do problems that take hours—or days—of debugging excite me rather than frustrate me?
  • Am I curious about why an algorithm works, not just how to code it?
  • Can I commit to continuous learning, even outside class hours?

If you answered "no" to any item

Discuss alternatives with an academic advisor before registering. It's better to choose the right path now than struggle later.
