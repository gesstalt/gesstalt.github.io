What Computer Science and Design Actually Involves Here

Forget traditional design programs. This program combines the complete Computer Science curriculum with design thinking and applications. You'll take ALL the same CS courses as regular CS students, with identical rigor and expectations.

Complete CS Core

Algorithms, data structures, programming, software engineering, computer systems

Master all fundamental CS concepts with the same depth and rigor as traditional CS majors.

Mathematical Foundations

Discrete mathematics, calculus, statistics, linear algebra, mathematical logic

Develop the same mathematical sophistication required for advanced computer science work.

Advanced CS Theory

Algorithm analysis, complexity theory, machine learning, artificial intelligence

Study theoretical computer science at the same advanced level as regular CS students.

Design Applications

Human-computer interaction, user experience, design thinking, visual computing

Apply CS knowledge to design problems, using computational methods to create user-centered solutions.

Program Focus: Computer Science First, Design Applications Second

This is a single interdisciplinary program with no major/minor options. The curriculum emphasis is:

80%

Computer Science Core

ALL CS subjects: algorithms, data structures, systems, theory, mathematics

Primary Focus: You'll take the complete CS curriculum with identical rigor and expectations as traditional CS students.

20%

Design Applications

HCI, UX design, visual computing, design thinking

Wrong Program? Consider This Instead

If your primary interest is Design and you want to learn some CS tools as supporting skills, you should look for our B.Design program (or take design as a Minor with Other Disciplines) with some technology components.

This program puts Computer Science first with Design as the application area.

Prerequisite Alert: Mathematics Foundation

Grade 12 Math = Absolutely Mandatory

Computer Science requires strong mathematical foundations. You'll be taking the same rigorous math courses as traditional CS students—no exceptions.

Required: Strong Grade 12 Mathematics Background

You need solid performance in Grade 12 calculus, algebra, and trigonometry. CS algorithms require mathematical analysis, discrete math needs logical reasoning, and advanced CS topics need calculus and statistics.

Same Standards as Traditional CS Students

You'll take identical CS courses alongside traditional CS students. There are no "easier" or "design-focused" versions of algorithms, data structures, or systems courses. The mathematical rigor and programming demands are exactly the same.

First-Year Course Pathways

First-year structure: You can take 4 courses total - 2 from Physical and Natural Sciences (PNS) and 2 CS courses requirements. CS courses are offered through PNS. Alongwith these four you must enroll for 2 more design courses.

Before selecting any other courses, understand this critical requirement:

MANDATORY CORE REQUIREMENT

CSIT104: Introduction to Computer Science

WITHOUT THIS COURSE, YOU CANNOT TAKE ANY SECOND-YEAR CS&D COURSES

Available in Term 2 and Term 4 - Choose one offering

Missing CSIT104 = No progression to CS&D Program

Strategic Course Selection Tips for CS&D

  • CS Foundation: CSIT104 is essential—it's the gateway to all advanced CS coursework in your program
  • Programming Progression: CSIT101 → CSIT111 gives you a strong programming foundation. Have Python experience? Jump to CSIT111
  • Mathematical Preparation: MATH151 (Introductory Calculus) is crucial for algorithm analysis and advanced CS theory
  • Logical Thinking: MATH155 (Mathematical Logic and Proofs) builds the reasoning skills needed for CS theory
  • Computational Applications: CSIT121 (Computational Modeling) shows how CS applies to design and visualization

Remember: This is Computer Science, Not Traditional Design

Your coursework will emphasize algorithms, programming, mathematical analysis, and systems thinking over traditional design methods. Expect the same level of technical rigor as traditional Computer Science programs.

Since CS courses are available through PNS, students committed to CS should take as many CS offerings as possible rather than spreading across other PNS options. This strategic selection gives you the strongest foundation for advanced CS coursework.

Self-Assessment Checklist

Answer yes to every question before declaring Computer Science and Design:

  • Did I excel in Grade 12 mathematics, including calculus and algebra?
  • Am I excited about programming and solving complex computational problems?
  • Do I want to learn the complete Computer Science curriculum, not just design tools?
  • Am I comfortable with the same mathematical and programming rigor as traditional CS students?
  • Am I more interested in how technology works than just how it looks?
  • Do I want to create innovative user experiences through deep technical understanding?
  • Can I handle algorithm analysis, data structures, and systems programming alongside design coursework?

If you answered "no" to any item

Consider traditional Design programs, Digital Media, or other fields. This CS&D program requires exceptional computational and mathematical readiness—there are no shortcuts to avoid the core CS curriculum.
