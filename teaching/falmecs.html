Is Computer Science Right for You?

Understanding what CS really involves at our university

Reality Check: Computer Science is far more than programming. Read everything below before making your decision.

What CS Actually Involves Here

Programming is just the tool, not the discipline. Our CS curriculum goes deep into theory, mathematics, systems, and data science.

Theory

Automata, computability, algorithm analysis, complexity classes

Forms the intellectual core of CS; lets you reason about what can be computed and how efficiently.

Mathematics & Statistics

Discrete math, proofs, linear algebra, probability, statistical inference

Underpins algorithms, machine learning, graphics, cryptography, and more.

Systems

Computer architecture, operating systems, networking, databases

Teaches you how hardware and software interact to create reliable, scalable platforms.

Programming Paradigms

Procedural, object-oriented, functional, concurrent

Gives you versatility to pick the right tool and style for each problem.

Major vs. Minor: The Relationship

Think of the minor as a well-chosen subset of the major, not a "watered-down" version.

Attribute CS Major CS Minor
Credits ~45–50 ~20–24
Depth of Theory Full sequence (discrete math → algorithms → advanced electives) Core theory only (discrete math + algorithms)
Systems Coverage Architecture, OS, networks, databases One systems course (choose any)
Electives 4–6 advanced topics of your choice 1–2 applied electives
Capstone / Research Required Not required
Career Trajectory Software engineer, research, grad school, startup founder Technical roles in other disciplines, data-savvy analyst, product manager

Prerequisite Alert: Mathematics

No Math Background = No CS Major or Minor

This is not a scare tactic—it is a realistic warning to prevent frustration and delayed graduation.

Required: Grade 12 Mathematics

Topics should include calculus, algebra/trigonometry, and introductory probability. Without this foundation you will struggle in first-year discrete math and calculus.

Why Math Matters in CS

Nearly every CS course builds on mathematical concepts. Algorithm analysis requires calculus and discrete math. Machine learning needs statistics and linear algebra. Graphics requires trigonometry and geometry.

First-Year Course Pathways

We offer a 4-term, 2-credit each bridge sequence to ease your transition:

Term 1: Computational Thinking

Problem decomposition, algorithm design, pseudocode, flowcharts

Term 2: Programming Foundations

Object-oriented design, debugging strategies, code organization

Term 3: Mathematical Reasoning

Discrete mathematics, proof techniques, set theory, logic

Term 4: Data Structures & Algorithms

Basic data structures, algorithm analysis, complexity theory

Self-Assessment Checklist

Answer yes to every question before declaring CS:

  • Did I enjoy and succeed in Grade 12 math?
  • Am I comfortable writing logical proofs or willing to learn quickly?
  • Do problems that take hours—or days—of debugging excite me rather than frustrate me?
  • Am I curious about why an algorithm works, not just how to code it?
  • Can I commit to continuous learning, even outside class hours?

If you answered "no" to any item

Discuss alternatives with an academic advisor before registering. It's better to choose the right path now than struggle later.
